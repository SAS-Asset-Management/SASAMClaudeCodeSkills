marcov.BEAM Selling Skill v1.0.0

Guide sales engagements through a structured, evidence-gated lifecycle. BEAM = Bayesian Evidence-Advancing Markov framework — a 6-stage pipeline where advancement requires earning the right through demonstrated evidence. No gut feel. No stage-skipping. Evidence or nothing.

6 Stages

From Qualify through Deliver — each stage has explicit gates and evidence requirements.

Evidence Gated

No advancing without meeting gate criteria. The skill is the gatekeeper, not the salesperson.

SPIN Integrated

Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff questioning woven into every stage.

Save & Resume

Session-to-session persistence via .beam/ directory. Pick up exactly where you left off.

Core Philosophy — Sales is a process of earning the right to advance. Each stage must be completed with concrete evidence before moving forward. The BEAM framework treats every engagement as a probabilistic model updated by real evidence.

BEAM Acronym

Each letter in BEAM maps to a foundational principle of the methodology:

LetterMeaningApplication
B Bayesian Update win probability with each new piece of evidence. Prior beliefs are refined, never ignored.
E Evidence Concrete evidence, not gut feel. Every gate requires demonstrable proof from prospect interactions.
A Advancing Forward movement only when earned. No stage-skipping, no premature proposals.
M Markov Current state determines next actions. History informs but the present stage dictates strategy.

Design Principles

Six non-negotiable principles govern every BEAM engagement:

  1. No stage-skipping. Each stage must be completed sequentially. There are no shortcuts — skipping Diagnose to jump to Propose guarantees a weak proposal.
  2. Skill is gatekeeper. The BEAM skill enforces stage transitions. The salesperson does not self-certify advancement — the evidence does.
  3. SPIN peaks in Stages 2–3. The heaviest questioning work happens during Diagnose and Align. By Propose, the prospect should already be selling themselves.
  4. “No” is legitimate. A qualified “no” is better than a delusional “maybe.” Disqualification is a valid and valuable outcome.
  5. Evidence over opinion. “I think they liked us” is worthless. “The CFO confirmed a $2M annual cost of the current process” is evidence.
  6. Earn the right. Every stage earns the right to the next. You earn the right to diagnose by qualifying. You earn the right to propose by aligning.

The 6-Stage Pipeline

The BEAM pipeline is a linear, evidence-gated progression. Each stage has defined entry criteria, activities, and exit gates.

1 Qualify → 2 Diagnose → 3 Align → 4 Propose → 5 Commit → 6 Deliver

Direction is forward only. If evidence reveals a gap, you address it within the current stage. Regression is a signal to re-evaluate, not to retreat.

Stage 1: Qualify

Purpose: Determine whether this opportunity is worth pursuing. Qualify out quickly if the fit is poor.

SPIN Focus

Situation — Understand the prospect’s context, current state, and environment.

Activities

  • Research the prospect’s industry, company, and key contacts
  • Identify the problem domain and whether it aligns with your offering
  • Determine access to authority (can you reach a decision-maker?)
  • Assess willingness to engage in a diagnostic conversation
  • Ingest B2B research dossier if available

Gate Criteria

GateRequirementStatus
Problem domain identifiedA clear, relevant problem domain has been articulatedRequired
Access to authorityPath to a decision-maker or strong influencer confirmedRequired
Willing to diagnoseProspect agrees to a diagnostic conversation or meetingRequired

Right to Advance

Meeting all three gates earns the right to Diagnose. Failing any gate means the opportunity is disqualified or parked until conditions change.

Minimum Evidence Bar

GateAcceptable EvidenceRejected Evidence
Problem domain identifiedSpecific problem statement from prospect communication, RFP, or industry research“They probably have this problem”
Access to authorityNamed contact with title, confirmed meeting or email thread“We know someone there”
Willing to diagnoseAccepted meeting invite, replied to diagnostic request, verbal agreement“They seemed interested”

Key Questions

  • What business is the prospect in and what are their primary challenges?
  • Who are the key stakeholders and what are their roles?
  • What systems, processes, or solutions do they currently use?
  • Is there a triggering event or timeline driving urgency?
  • What is the approximate budget or investment capacity?

Stage 2: Diagnose

Purpose: Deeply understand the prospect’s problems, their impact, and the cost of inaction. This is where SPIN questioning does its heaviest work.

SPIN Focus

Problem → Implication — Uncover difficulties, then develop their severity and business impact.

Activities

  • Conduct diagnostic meetings with key stakeholders
  • Map problems to business impact using quantifiable metrics
  • Build implication chains (problem → consequence → cost)
  • Document problems in the prospect’s own words
  • Quantify cost of inaction where possible
  • Validate findings with multiple stakeholders

Gate Criteria

GateRequirementStatus
Problems articulated by prospectProspect has stated problems in their own words, not parroted backRequired
Implications quantifiedAt least one problem has a measurable business impact (time, cost, risk)Required
Cost of inaction acknowledgedProspect acknowledges what happens if nothing changesRequired

Minimum Evidence Bar

GateAcceptable EvidenceRejected Evidence
Problems articulated by prospectDirect quotes from meetings, email confirmations, recorded statements“They nodded when I described the problem”
Implications quantified“The CFO confirmed $2M annual cost”, “3 FTEs dedicated to manual workarounds”“It’s costing them a lot”
Cost of inaction acknowledgedProspect statement: “If we don’t address this, we risk X”“They know it’s bad”

SPIN Questions — Problem

  • What difficulties are you experiencing with your current approach?
  • How satisfied are you with the existing process/system?
  • What workarounds has your team developed?
  • Where do errors or failures most commonly occur?
  • What limitations are preventing you from achieving your goals?

SPIN Questions — Implication

  • What effect does this problem have on overall productivity?
  • How does this impact your ability to meet deadlines or targets?
  • What is the financial cost of this issue annually?
  • How does this affect your team’s morale or retention?
  • If this problem worsens, what are the consequences for the business?
  • Does this create risk in other areas of the organisation?

Stage 3: Align

Purpose: Agree on the problem definition, success criteria, and decision process. By the end of Align, both parties should have a shared understanding of what “solved” looks like.

SPIN Focus

Implication → Need-payoff — Transition from pain to desired outcomes. Let the prospect articulate the value.

Activities

  • Co-create a shared problem definition document
  • Define measurable success criteria with the prospect
  • Map the stakeholder landscape (decision-makers, influencers, blockers)
  • Understand the decision process (timeline, stages, approvals)
  • Identify budget parameters and procurement requirements
  • Align on evaluation criteria

Gate Criteria

GateRequirementStatus
Problem definition agreedBoth parties have a written, agreed problem statementRequired
Success criteria definedMeasurable outcomes that define “solved” are documentedRequired
Stakeholder landscape mappedAll key stakeholders identified with roles and influence levelsRequired
Decision process understoodTimeline, approval steps, and evaluation criteria are knownRequired

Minimum Evidence Bar

GateAcceptable EvidenceRejected Evidence
Problem definition agreedSigned-off problem statement, email confirmation of shared document“We’re on the same page”
Success criteria definedWritten KPIs: “Reduce processing time by 40%”, “Eliminate manual reconciliation”“They want it to be better”
Stakeholder landscape mappedNamed individuals with titles, roles (champion, blocker, approver), and contact details“A few people are involved”
Decision process understoodDocumented timeline, approval chain, budget holder identified“They’ll decide soon”

SPIN Questions — Need-payoff

  • If you could solve this problem, what would that mean for your team?
  • How would reducing [specific cost] impact your annual budget?
  • What would it be worth to eliminate [specific problem]?
  • How would your stakeholders react to [specific outcome]?
  • What other benefits would come from solving this?
  • Would a solution that achieves [X] justify the investment?

Stage 4: Propose

Purpose: Present a tailored proposal that directly addresses the agreed problems, success criteria, and stakeholder needs. No surprises — the proposal should feel like a natural conclusion to Stages 1–3.

SPIN Focus

Need-payoff — Reinforce the value. The prospect should already be advocating internally by this stage.

Activities

  • Draft proposal using the proposal template
  • Map every proposal element back to a diagnosed problem
  • Include success criteria from the Align stage
  • Address known objections proactively
  • Present commercial terms clearly (pricing, timeline, deliverables)
  • Confirm the decision process and next steps
  • Socialise the proposal with the champion before formal presentation

Gate Criteria

GateRequirementStatus
Proposal addresses diagnosed needsEvery proposal element traces to a specific diagnosed problemRequired
Commercial terms clearPricing, scope, timeline, and deliverables are unambiguousRequired
Decision process confirmedNext steps, timeline, and approvers re-confirmed post-proposalRequired
Objections addressedKnown concerns have been surfaced and resolved or mitigatedRequired

Minimum Evidence Bar

GateAcceptable EvidenceRejected Evidence
Proposal addresses diagnosed needsTraceability matrix linking proposal sections to diagnosed problems“The proposal covers everything”
Commercial terms clearWritten proposal with pricing table, scope document, and timelineVerbal pricing discussion only
Decision process confirmedEmail from prospect confirming next steps and timeline“They said they’ll get back to us”
Objections addressedDocumented objection log with resolution notes“They didn’t raise any concerns”

Stage 5: Commit

Purpose: Secure formal commitment — contract signature, purchase order, or equivalent binding agreement.

Activities

  • Facilitate final negotiations and contract review
  • Support champion through internal approval process
  • Address any last-minute objections or concerns
  • Coordinate legal and procurement requirements
  • Confirm implementation timeline and kick-off logistics
  • Obtain signed contract or equivalent commitment

Gate Criteria

GateRequirementStatus
Contract signedSigned agreement, purchase order, or equivalent binding commitment receivedRequired

Commitment is binary. Either you have a signed contract or you do not. Verbal commitments, handshakes, and “we’ll sign next week” are not commitment. Stay in Stage 5 until ink is on paper.

Stage 6: Deliver & Renew

Purpose: Execute on the commitment, deliver value, and lay the groundwork for renewal, expansion, or referral.

Activities

  • Conduct kick-off meeting with implementation team and client
  • Deliver against agreed scope, timeline, and success criteria
  • Provide regular progress updates to stakeholders
  • Measure outcomes against the success criteria from Stage 3
  • Gather testimonials and case study material
  • Identify expansion opportunities and new problem domains
  • Initiate renewal conversations before contract expiry

Success Criteria

CriterionMeasure
Value deliveredSuccess criteria from Stage 3 met or exceeded
Client satisfactionPositive feedback, NPS score, or testimonial obtained
Renewal pipelineExpansion or renewal opportunity identified and entered into pipeline
Reference potentialClient willing to serve as reference or provide case study

SPIN Questioning

SPIN is the questioning engine that powers the BEAM pipeline. Each question type has a specific purpose and peaks at different stages.

Situation

Understand the prospect’s context, current state, processes, and environment. Peaks in Stage 1.

Problem

Uncover difficulties, dissatisfactions, and challenges the prospect faces. Peaks in Stage 2.

Implication

Develop the severity and business impact of problems. Make the cost of inaction tangible. Peaks in Stage 2–3.

Need-payoff

Let the prospect articulate the value of a solution. They sell themselves. Peaks in Stage 3–4.

Example Situation Questions

  • How is your team currently handling [process]?
  • What tools or systems do you use for [function]?
  • How many people are involved in [activity]?
  • What does your typical workflow look like?

Example Problem Questions

  • What are the biggest challenges with your current approach?
  • Where do things most commonly break down?
  • How often do errors or delays occur?
  • What frustrates your team about the current process?

Example Implication Questions

  • When errors occur, how long does it take to recover?
  • What is the downstream impact on [revenue / customers / compliance]?
  • How does this affect your ability to scale?
  • What happens if this problem gets worse over the next 12 months?

Example Need-payoff Questions

  • If you could eliminate [problem], what would that free your team to focus on?
  • How would [specific improvement] impact your KPIs?
  • What would it be worth to your organisation to achieve [outcome]?
  • Would solving this problem make it easier to achieve [strategic goal]?

Evidence Gating

Evidence gating is the mechanism that prevents premature stage advancement. The BEAM skill acts as an impartial gatekeeper.

How It Works

All gates met with acceptable evidence
Advance to next stage
Some gates met, others pending
Hold — identify specific actions to close gaps
Evidence contradicts viability
Disqualify or park the opportunity

The skill never rubber-stamps. If the salesperson asserts a gate is met, the skill will ask for the specific evidence. “They seemed positive” is not evidence. A direct quote, an email, or a confirmed metric is evidence.

Probability Updates

Win probability is updated at each stage transition using Bayesian reasoning. The probability reflects the cumulative evidence strength.

StageTypical Probability RangeKey Evidence Driver
1. Qualify5–15%Problem-fit and access confirmed
2. Diagnose15–30%Problems quantified, cost of inaction acknowledged
3. Align30–50%Shared problem definition, success criteria agreed
4. Propose50–75%Proposal accepted, objections resolved
5. Commit75–95%Contract in negotiation or signature pending
6. Deliver100%Contract signed, engagement active

Probabilities are not fixed. They are updated based on evidence quality. A Stage 3 deal with weak evidence may sit at 30%, while a Stage 3 deal with strong multi-stakeholder alignment may reach 50%. The framework is Bayesian, not formulaic.

State Persistence

BEAM engagements persist across sessions using a .beam/ directory in the project root. This allows you to resume any engagement exactly where you left off.

Directory Structure

      evidence-log.md        # Timestamped evidence entries
      stakeholder-map.md     # Stakeholder landscape
      meeting-notes/         # Individual meeting summaries

  config.json                # Skill configuration

Kanban Board

The kanban board provides a pipeline-wide view of all active engagements, organised by stage. It is stored at .beam/kanban.md and updated automatically as engagements progress.

QualifyDiagnoseAlignProposeCommitDeliver
New leads awaiting qualification Active diagnostic conversations Problem & criteria alignment Proposals out for review Contracts in negotiation Active engagements

Engagement Tracker

The engagement tracker provides a detailed view of each individual engagement, including stage history, evidence log, stakeholder map, and next actions. Stored per-engagement at .beam/engagements/{slug}/.

Key Metrics Tracked

  • Current stage and time-in-stage
  • Win probability (Bayesian-updated)
  • Gates met vs. gates remaining
  • Last interaction date and next scheduled action
  • Stakeholder engagement level
  • Evidence quality score per gate

B2B Research Agent

The BEAM selling skill integrates with the b2b-research-agent skill for automated prospect research. When a B2B research dossier is available, the BEAM skill auto-ingests it during Stage 1 qualification.

Integration Flow

B2B dossier available
Auto-ingest into engagement context during Qualify
No dossier available
Manual research using BEAM’s own qualification questions

What Gets Ingested

  • Company overview, size, industry, and key financials
  • Key contacts and stakeholder profiles
  • Recent news, initiatives, and strategic priorities
  • Competitive landscape and market position
  • Technology stack and infrastructure indicators

Input & Commands

The BEAM skill is invoked through natural language or specific command patterns. The skill recognises context from the conversation and engagement state.

Invocation Examples

# Start a new engagement
"Start a new BEAM engagement for Acme Corp"
"Begin qualifying Acme Corp"

# Resume an existing engagement
"Resume the Acme Corp engagement"
"Where are we with Acme Corp?"

# Update evidence
"Log evidence: CFO confirmed $2M annual cost of manual reconciliation"
"Gate update: problem domain identified — they need asset lifecycle management"

# Check status
"Show pipeline"
"What stage is Acme Corp at?"
"Show gate status for Acme Corp"

# Request guidance
"What questions should I ask in the next meeting?"
"What evidence do I still need for Stage 2?"
"Help me prepare for the diagnostic meeting"

Dossier Auto-Ingestion

When the b2b-research-agent skill has produced a dossier for a prospect, BEAM will detect it automatically and offer to ingest the findings. This pre-populates Situation context and accelerates Stage 1 qualification.

# If a dossier exists at .b2b/dossiers/acme-corp.md:
"Start BEAM engagement for Acme Corp"
# → Skill detects dossier and prompts:
# "B2B dossier found for Acme Corp. Ingest into engagement? [Y/n]"
