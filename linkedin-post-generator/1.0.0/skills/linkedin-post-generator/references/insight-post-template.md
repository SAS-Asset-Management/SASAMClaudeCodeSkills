# Quick Insight Post Template

Use this template for standalone thought leadership posts. These don't link to an article — they deliver value directly and drive engagement through comments and discussion.

## Structure

```
[HOOK — single powerful line that stops the scroll]

[INSIGHT — the main point in 2-3 sentences]

[EVIDENCE or EXAMPLE — brief, concrete support]

[SO WHAT — why this matters to the reader's work]

[CTA — question or prompt that invites discussion]
```

## Post Types

Pick the best fit for your topic:

1. **Single actionable tip** — one thing the reader can do this week
2. **Contrarian take** — challenge a common assumption in asset management
3. **Myth-busting** — name the myth, explain why it's wrong, give the reality
4. **Quick list** — 3-5 items (signs, mistakes, lessons, tips)
5. **Lesson learned** — personal reflection from real project experience
6. **Conference snippet** — one takeaway from a talk, panel, or conversation

## Rules

- Length: 150-400 words
- Must stand alone — no dependency on an external article
- One clear takeaway — don't try to cover everything
- End with engagement: a question, a challenge, or a prompt
- Be specific — name the standard, the metric, the technique
- Acknowledge trade-offs — "this works well when X, less so when Y"
- No emojis
- No hashtags
- Australian English spelling

## Example: Contrarian Take

```
Stop calling it predictive maintenance if you're just running it to failure with a dashboard.

In practice, what most organisations call "predictive" is actually condition monitoring with no decision logic attached.

Real predictive maintenance means:
→ Failure modes are modelled, not just monitored
→ Algorithms recommend actions BEFORE thresholds are breached
→ Maintenance schedules adapt based on asset health, not calendar intervals

The difference matters. One gives you data. The other gives you decisions.

Worth noting: you don't need perfect data to start. You need the right failure modes, a decent sensor strategy, and someone who understands reliability engineering — not just data science.

What's your organisation actually doing — predictive, or just monitoring with extra steps?
```

## Example: Quick List

```
5 things I wish someone told me before our first AI pilot in asset management:

1. Your CMMS data quality matters more than your model architecture
2. The maintenance team needs to trust the output — or they'll ignore it
3. Start with one failure mode on one critical asset, not a fleet-wide rollout
4. "Proof of concept" and "production-ready" are separated by 6-12 months of engineering
5. The business case isn't "AI is cool" — it's "here's the dollar value of fewer unplanned failures"

The honest answer is that most AI pilots succeed technically and fail organisationally.

The ones that stick? They start with a problem the maintenance team already cares about, and they prove value in weeks, not quarters.

What would you add to this list?
```

## Example: Single Actionable Tip

```
Here's a quick win for any asset manager drowning in maintenance backlogs.

Sort your backlog by criticality, not by age.

Most CMMS systems default to showing the oldest work orders first. That makes sense for a queue, but not for risk.

A 6-month-old PM on a non-critical asset matters less than a 2-week-old defect on something that shuts down operations if it fails.

In practice:
→ Tag every work order with the asset's criticality rating
→ Sort by criticality first, then by due date within each tier
→ Review the top 20 weekly with your planning team

You'll close fewer work orders. But you'll close the ones that matter.

How does your team prioritise the backlog?
```
